Discover Indian Art Forms

This page is part of the game created by Team Vriddhi, NPS Jayanagar, Bengaluru — a guided tour of India's performing, visual, and craft traditions that appear in the game.

Overview

India's art forms are diverse, regionally rooted, and full of stories, techniques, and values. In the game, players encounter challenges inspired by dance, music, crafts, theatre, and folk traditions. Each encounter aims to teach cultural context while encouraging strategic thinking and teamwork.

Types of art forms (click to explore)

1. Mandala

Mandala art is a symbolic circular design that originated in Hindu and Buddhist traditions to represent the universe's wholeness and interconnectedness. The term “mandala” means “circle” in Sanskrit. These artworks typically feature intricate geometric patterns radiating from a central point and were traditionally used for meditation and sacred rituals. Creating mandalas is popular today for its meditative and therapeutic benefits, promoting focus, mindfulness, and relaxation.

2. Madhubani (Mithila)

A folk art tradition from Bihar’s Mithila region, characterized by intricate line work and vibrant fills. Traditionally painted on mud walls and floors, it is now created on paper, cloth, and canvas using fingers, twigs, brushes, and nib pens. Themes span mythology, nature, and daily life, featuring motifs like deities, animals, flowers, and geometric patterns, often coloured with natural dyes derived from turmeric, henna, and charcoal.

3. Gond

A tribal art form from the Gond community of Central India known for its intricate patterns of dots and lines, vibrant colours and imaginative depictions of nature, mythology, and folklore. Traditionally it was created on mud walls using natural materials like charcoal, clay and plant pigments. Modern Gond art is still created with these traditional methods and natural colors but is also frequently produced on paper and canvas using acrylic paints.

4. Kalamkari

An ancient Indian textile art from Andhra Pradesh that involves handpainting or block printing intricate designs on cotton or silk fabric using natural dyes. The name comes from the Persian words kalam (pen) and kari (craftsmanship), referencing the use of a tamarind pen for freehand drawing.

5. Pichwai

A traditional art form from Rajasthan consisting of intricate cloth paintings that traditionally served as backdrops for the idol of Srinathji, a form of Lord Krishna. The word Pichwai comes from Pich (back) and Wai (hanging). The art features vibrant colors and detailed depictions of scenes from Lord Krishna’s life, often with Gopis, Cows, and Lotuses.
